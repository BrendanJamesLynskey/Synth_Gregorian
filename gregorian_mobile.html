About Gregorian Chant

Plainsong • c. 9th–10th century • Frankish–Roman

Gregorian chant is the monophonic, unaccompanied sacred song of the Western Latin Church — a single melodic line, sung in free rhythm to Latin scripture, with no harmony and no fixed beat. It crystallised in the Carolingian empire from a fusion of Roman and Gallican traditions and was later credited (legendarily) to Pope Gregory I.

Its melodies move mostly by step within one of eight church modes, rising from a finalis to recite on a tenor (reciting tone) before cadencing home. Text is set syllabically or spun into long melismatic neumes. This engine recreates the singing voice itself with the shared FOF vocal-synthesis library (vocal-voices.js) — the IRCAM CHANT method, a burst of overlapping formant grains per glottal pulse.

Where it sits in the lineage

Plainchant is the root of all later Western music. Add a second voice to it and you get organum; layer and organise those voices rhythmically and you reach the polyphony of Notre Dame and the Ars Nova. Its melodies also became the fixed cantus firmus beneath centuries of polyphony.
